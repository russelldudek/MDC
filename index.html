The real job

This is not a generic COO seat. It is a founder-leverage and services-economics mandate.

01

Sell bespoke. Deliver repeatably.

Client work must remain specific enough to matter while the delivery system becomes predictable enough to quote, staff, and improve.

02

Use agents. Keep accountability human.

AI systems are managed capacity: they need an owner, evaluation standard, escalation path, cost envelope, and operating review.

03

Move fast. Protect margin.

Urgency cannot become scope ambiguity, bench panic, escalation debt, or forecasts that look precise only until actuals arrive.

04

Remove the founder. Preserve the founder's signal.

The founder's market instinct should shape the system without remaining the system's daily routing layer.

Operating thesis

Every engagement needs an operating contract from commitment to renewal.

The COO should make six operating contracts explicit before work can scale: what was promised, what is bounded, what capacity can execute, how the work is controlled, what the client will accept, and what learning changes the next engagement.

Discovery hypothesis: the highest-leverage first move is one shared Engagement Operating Record that sales, delivery, finance, and AI-system owners use to make the same decision.

  1. CommitmentWhat measurable client outcome was actually promised?Owner: sales lead · Evidence: signed outcome language
  2. ScopeWhat is bounded, excluded, priced, and change-controlled?Owner: COO · Evidence: scope contract and assumptions
  3. CapacityWhat human-and-agent mix can execute at target economics?Owner: delivery lead · Evidence: staffing and utilization plan
  4. ExecutionWhat cadence exposes risk before schedule and margin drift?Owner: engagement lead · Evidence: weekly delivery record
  5. EvidenceWhat will the client accept as an outcome, not an activity report?Owner: client decision owner · Evidence: acceptance receipt
  6. RenewalWhat learning changes the next price, proposal, staffing model, and agent?Owner: COO · Evidence: renewal and closeout review

Entry posture

Instrument first. Stabilize second. Scale only what the evidence can carry.

0-30

Read the system

Map founder dependencies, engagement economics, pipeline handoffs, agent jobs, current scorecards, and the client evidence chain.

31-60

Close the gaps

Install one engagement record, explicit escalation thresholds, scope-change control, forecast reconciliation, and a founder/COO working agreement.

61-90

Build the bench

Connect capacity planning to pipeline confidence; standardize remote onboarding; make agent owners and evaluations visible.

91-120

Release attention

Run the company rhythm without the founder, validate renewal and margin evidence, and select the next workflow for agent-supported redesign.

Executive conversation

The questions I would use to test the mandate.

01Where does the founder still become the operating system today - deal shaping, staffing, escalation, pricing, client confidence, or all five?

02Which engagement outcome most reliably predicts renewal, and how consistently is that outcome documented before closeout?

03Where do quoted assumptions most often diverge from delivery actuals: scope, seniority mix, agent leverage, client readiness, or change burden?

04Which internal agent is currently most valuable, and what evidence would justify treating it as dependable capacity rather than a useful tool?

05What decision should the COO make independently by day 30 that still routes through the founder today?
